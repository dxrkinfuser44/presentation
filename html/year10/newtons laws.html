Year 10 Physics

Newton's Laws of Motion

Three foundational principles that describe how objects move, accelerate, and interact through forces.

First Law Second Law Third Law
01
Newton's First Law
The Law of Inertia
The Law
An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion continues at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.
v = const. when Fnet = 0
Application
Seatbelts in cars. When a car brakes suddenly, your body keeps moving forward. The seatbelt provides the external force to bring you to a stop, preventing injury.
Analogy
A hockey puck on ice keeps sliding long after you stop pushing, because friction is very low. On carpet, the larger friction force brings it to a stop quickly.
Practical Demo
Variables
IV: speed of card flick   DV: whether coin falls in   CV: coin mass, surface
Equipment
Playing card, coin, empty cup or glass
Method
Balance the card over the cup rim. Place a coin on top. Flick the card sharply sideways. The coin's inertia keeps it stationary while the card moves, so it drops straight into the cup.
02
Newton's Second Law
Force and Acceleration
The Law
Acceleration is directly proportional to the net force applied, and inversely proportional to the object's mass.
F = ma
Application
Shopping trolleys. A fully loaded trolley needs much more force than an empty one to reach the same speed. Double the mass means double the force for equal acceleration.
Analogy
Kick a soccer ball and it flies across the field. Kick a bowling ball with the same force and it barely shifts. Same kick, very different mass, dramatically different acceleration.
Practical Demo
Variables
IV: number of rubber bands   DV: cart acceleration   CV: cart mass, surface type
Equipment
Toy car or dynamics cart, 3 identical rubber bands, metre ruler, stopwatch or phone slow-motion
Method
Stretch 1 rubber band to a fixed mark and release the cart. Measure distance in 2 seconds. Repeat with 2 and 3 bands. Record and compare acceleration across trials.
03
Newton's Third Law
Action and Reaction
The Law
For every action force there is an equal and opposite reaction force. Forces always occur in pairs, acting on two different objects simultaneously.
FA on B = −FB on A
Application
Rocket propulsion. Burning fuel expels gas downward at high speed (action). The rocket receives an equal upward force (reaction). This works in the vacuum of space, requiring no ground to push against.
Analogy
Stand on a skateboard and push off a wall. You push the wall forward; the wall pushes you backward with equal force. You roll away from the wall without touching the ground at all.
Practical Demo
Variables
IV: mass of each participant   DV: distance each person travels   CV: push force, floor surface
Equipment
Two wheeled chairs or skateboards, smooth floor, measuring tape, two students of different masses
Method
Sit facing each other on chairs. One person pushes the other. Both move apart. The lighter student travels farther, connecting to F = ma alongside the reaction pair.
